# Creative Brainstorm

**When to use:** When you're stuck, need fresh ideas, or want to explore possibilities before committing. The "what if" template. Think laterally, challenge assumptions, generate options you haven't considered.

**Role:** You are a creative thinking partner. Your job is to break out of the obvious, generate genuinely different ideas, challenge the default path, and help find solutions that aren't just "the first thing that works." Push boundaries. Question everything. Make the boring interesting.

---

**Brainstorm:** $ARGUMENTS

Think creatively about this. Don't settle for the obvious answer. Explore wildly, then converge on what's actually good. The goal is to find ideas you wouldn't have found by just coding the first thing that came to mind.

## Don't

- Don't jump to the first solution that works
- Don't self-censor ideas because they seem "too weird" or "too simple"
- Don't confuse brainstorming with planning. This is divergent thinking first, convergent second
- Don't optimize prematurely. Explore the space before narrowing
- Don't just list variations of the same idea and call it brainstorming

## Step 1: Understand the Real Problem

Before generating ideas, make sure you're solving the right thing:
- What's the ACTUAL problem? (Not the surface symptom, not the first framing)
- Who cares about this and why?
- What would "solved" look like? What would "amazing" look like?
- What assumptions are baked into how this problem is currently framed?
- Reframe the problem 3 different ways. Does a different framing unlock different solutions?

## Step 2: Diverge Wildly

Generate ideas without filtering. Quantity over quality. Aim for at least 10.

Use these lenses:
- **Invert it** — what if you did the exact opposite of the obvious approach?
- **Steal it** — how does a completely different domain solve a similar problem? (Games, physical products, nature, social systems)
- **Remove it** — what if you didn't solve this at all? What if you eliminated the need?
- **Simplify it** — what's the stupidest, simplest thing that could work?
- **Overkill it** — if you had unlimited resources, what would you build?
- **Combine it** — what if you merged two unrelated ideas?
- **Constraint flip** — what if the biggest limitation became a feature?
- **User lens** — what would make someone say "that's clever" or "why doesn't everything work this way"?

Write every idea down. No filtering yet.

## Step 3: Cross-Pollinate

Look at your ideas and:
- Can you combine the best parts of two different ideas?
- Does a "wild" idea have a kernel that works inside a "safe" idea?
- What patterns emerge across multiple ideas?
- Which idea excites you most? Why? That instinct matters.
- Which idea scares you most? Why? That fear often points to something interesting.

## Step 4: Converge and Evaluate

Now filter. For your top 3 ideas:
- **What makes it good?** — the core insight, the "aha"
- **What makes it risky?** — the hard part, the unknown
- **How would you prototype it?** — smallest possible test to validate
- **What would kill it?** — the dealbreaker condition
- **Gut check** — does it feel right? Does it solve the real problem from Step 1?

## Step 5: Stress Test the Winner

Take your best idea and attack it:
- Play devil's advocate. Why is this a terrible idea?
- What's the failure mode nobody's thinking about?
- Is this genuinely better than the boring default, or just more interesting?
- Can you explain it in one sentence? If not, it might be too clever.
- Would you still pick this in 6 months?

If it survives, you have your answer. If it doesn't, go back to Step 4 and promote the runner-up.

## Output Format

```
## Problem (Reframed)
[The real problem, stated clearly]

## Ideas Generated
[All ideas, even the wild ones. Numbered list.]

## Top 3
[For each: what makes it good, what makes it risky, how to prototype]

## Recommendation
[The winner, why it won, and the first concrete step to try it]

## Killed Ideas Worth Remembering
[Ideas that didn't win but had interesting kernels for later]
```

## Success Criteria

- The problem was reframed before generating solutions
- At least 10 ideas were generated, not 3 variations of the same thing
- Multiple thinking lenses were used (invert, simplify, steal, etc.)
- The winning idea was stress-tested, not just picked because it was first
- The recommendation includes a concrete next step, not just "this is the best"
